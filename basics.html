Diabetes Basics


Diabetes mellitus (DM) is a chronic disease with a major impact on morbidity and mortality as well as socioeconomics. This impact is because of its high prevalence and incidence as well as the associated acute and chronic complications, which are caused by poor glucose control. Therefore, self-management of blood glucose (BG) is the standard of care for people with diabetes.
On the basis of previous studies, it has been found that for those with type 2 diabetes (T2DM) BG can be maintained through physical activity, healthy diet, and weight loss. For people with type 1 diabetes (T1DM), it is more difficult to control through diet and exercise.


Symptomatology


Symptoms of diabetes include:
     
  • Increased thirst and urination

  • Increased appetit

  • Fatigueatigue

  • Blurred vision

  • Numbness or tingling in the hands or feet

  • Ulcers that do not heal

  • Weight loss for no apparent reason


    • Symptoms of type 1 diabetes can appear quickly, within weeks. Symptoms of type 2 diabetes, on the other hand, usually progress very slowly over several years and can be so mild that sometimes they are not even noticed. Many people with type 2 diabetes have no symptoms. Some only find out they have the disease when diabetes-related health problems arise, such as blurred vision or heart problems.


Diagnosis


Your doctor, or other healthcare professional, can diagnose diabetes, pre-diabetes, and gestational diabetes through blood tests. Blood tests show if your blood glucose level, also called blood sugar, is too high.

Don't try to diagnose yourself if you think you may have diabetes. Over-the-counter equipment, such as glucometers, cannot diagnose diabetes.

Anyone who has symptoms of diabetes should be screened for the disease. Some people will not have symptoms, but they may have risk factors for diabetes and should be tested. Tests allow health professionals to detect diabetes earlier and work with their patients to manage the disease and prevent its complications.

The tests also allow health professionals to detect pre-diabetes. Lifestyle changes aimed at losing a moderate amount of weight, if overweight, can help delay or prevent type 2 diabetes.
